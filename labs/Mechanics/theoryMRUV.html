How calculus really describes motion

In kinematics we often see three compact formulas: \( v = \dfrac{dx}{dt} \), \( a = \dfrac{dv}{dt} \) and \( a = v \dfrac{dv}{dx} \). This page connects them to intuition and to the Uniformly Accelerated Motion Lab (MRUV) in this portfolio.

Reading guide

Think of a single object moving along a straight line (1D motion). We’ll build the story in three layers:

1. Position → Velocity 2. Velocity → Acceleration (in time) 3. Acceleration as a function of position

1. Position and velocity: \( v = \dfrac{dx}{dt} \)

Position as a function of time
Concept

If an object moves along a line, we describe its position with a function x(t), where:

  • x is the position (for example, in meters).
  • t is time (seconds).
  • The graph of x(t) is a curve in the plane position vs. time.
Idea: zoom in on the curve. Very close to a point, the curve looks almost like a straight line. The slope of that tiny line is the instantaneous velocity.
Velocity as a derivative
Definition

The instantaneous velocity is defined as:

\( v(t) = \dfrac{dx}{dt} \)

  • “Change of position per unit time”.
  • Units: meters per second (m/s).
  • Geometrically: the slope of the tangent line to the graph of x(t).
When to use it?
Whenever you know or assume the function x(t). Differentiate it to get v(t).

2. Velocity and acceleration in time: \( a = \dfrac{dv}{dt} \)

How fast does the velocity change?
Concept

If velocity tells us how position changes, acceleration tells us how velocity changes:

\( a(t) = \dfrac{dv}{dt} \)

  • Units: m/s² (change of m/s per second).
  • If a > 0, speed increases in the positive direction.
  • If a < 0, the object is slowing down (or speeding up in the opposite direction).
Geometric view: On the velocity–time graph, acceleration is the slope of the curve.
Uniformly accelerated motion (MRUV)
Special case

In MRUV the acceleration is constant:

a(t) = a_0

Integrating step by step:

  • \( v(t) = v_0 + a_0 t \)
  • \( x(t) = x_0 + v_0 t + \dfrac{1}{2} a_0 t^2 \)

These are the classic MRUV equations you see in problem 11.3 and that we use inside the interactive MRUV Lab.

3. Acceleration as a function of position: \( a = v \, \dfrac{dv}{dx} \)

Chain rule connection
Why this formula appears

Sometimes the force (and therefore the acceleration) depends more naturally on the position of the object than on time. In that case, we use the chain rule from calculus to connect a = dv/dt with derivatives with respect to x.

start a = dv/dt
chain rule dv/dt = (dv/dx) · (dx/dt)
but dx/dt = v
therefore a = v · dv/dx

So the same acceleration can be written in three equivalent ways:

timea = dv/dt positiona = v · dv/dx
When to use \( a = v \, dv/dx \)?
  • When you know or can express the force as a function of position F(x).
  • In energy methods: relating kinetic and potential energy.
  • When it is easier to integrate with respect to x than with respect to t.
Mini example (idea, not full solution)

Imagine a particle moving in a potential well where the force is F(x) = -k x (like a spring). From Newton: m a = -k x.

If we write a = v dv/dx, then m v dv/dx = -k x, which is a differential equation in variables v and x. Integrating gives directly a relation between velocity and position.

4. Summary: which equation should I use?

The textbook paragraph you showed basically says: “here is a summary of how and when to use each equation”. Here is that idea in a compact, visual form:

  • If you know \( x(t) \):
    use \( v = \dfrac{dx}{dt} \) to get velocity, then \( a = \dfrac{dv}{dt} \) to get acceleration.
  • If acceleration is constant:
    integrate once to get v(t), integrate again to get x(t) (MRUV equations).
  • If you know \( a(x) \) or \( F(x) \):
    write a = v dv/dx and integrate with respect to x to find how velocity depends on position.
  • Graphically:
    – slope of x(t) → v(t)
    – slope of v(t) → a(t)
